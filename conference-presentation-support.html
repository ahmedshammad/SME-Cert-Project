Section 1

1Abstract

▶Slide 1–2: Opening slide. Read verbatim or paraphrase as a 60-second spoken abstract before the title slide appears.

The proliferation of fraudulent and unverifiable credentials presents a systemic risk to Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) operating within regulated economic ecosystems. Existing certificate management approaches — whether paper-based or reliant on centralised digital registries — are inherently susceptible to forgery, single points of failure, and the absence of real-time revocability. This research proposes, designs, implements, and evaluates a permissioned blockchain-based credential management framework tailored to the needs of the Egyptian SME sector and aligned with Egypt Vision 2030.

The proposed system, designated the SME Certificate Trust Platform, leverages Hyperledger Fabric 2.5 as the underlying distributed ledger infrastructure, orchestrating four independent issuing organisations — representing government ministries, development agencies, training providers, and external auditors — across a Raft-consensus network of three ordering nodes and eight endorsing peers. Certificate payloads are encrypted via AES-256-GCM envelope encryption and persisted in a decentralised InterPlanetary File System (IPFS) node, while only cryptographic metadata — including SHA-256 content hashes, ECDSA P-256 signatures, and pseudonymous holder identifiers — are committed to the immutable ledger. Role-based access control spanning seven distinct actor classes is enforced at both the chaincode and application layers.

The platform is fully containerised, production-deployed on Oracle Cloud Infrastructure, and validated against the W3C Verifiable Credentials Data Model. Empirical evaluation demonstrates sub-second verification latency, robust revocation propagation, and compliance with GDPR-aligned data minimisation principles. The system constitutes a reproducible, open-architecture contribution to the emerging discipline of blockchain-mediated digital trust infrastructure for developing economies.

2Problem Statement

▶Slides 3–5: Use one slide per sub-problem. Bullet points + a single compelling statistic per slide drives engagement.

2.1 The Credential Trust Crisis in Egyptian SMEs

Egyptian Small and Medium Enterprises constitute approximately 75% of non-oil GDP and employ over 80% of the private sector workforce, yet they operate within a credential ecosystem characterised by pervasive informality and institutional mistrust. Professional qualifications, quality certifications, regulatory compliance documents, and government-issued permits are routinely issued in paper form, rendering them susceptible to physical degradation, duplication, and deliberate falsification. Recipients lack a portable, machine-verifiable identity layer; verifiers lack a scalable mechanism for authenticating credentials without contacting the original issuing authority.

2.2 Limitations of Centralised Digital Approaches

Prior digitisation efforts in the region have produced centralised certificate portals — single-server architectures in which the issuing body maintains the sole authoritative record. Such designs inherit structural vulnerabilities:

  • Single point of failure: Server compromise, database corruption, or organisational dissolution permanently destroys the trust anchor for all previously issued certificates.
  • Absence of cross-organisational verifiability: Certificates issued by Ministry A cannot be verified by an employer querying Ministry B's portal without bilateral API agreements.
  • Revocation opacity: Revoked credentials continue to circulate in printed form indefinitely; there is no mechanism for instant, irrevocable invalidation accessible to all parties.
  • Privacy risks: Centralised databases aggregating holder personal data present high-value targets for data exfiltration and identity fraud.

2.3 Policy Context: Egypt Vision 2030

Egypt's national development strategy explicitly targets the digitalisation of government services, the formalisation of the SME sector, and the establishment of a knowledge-based economy. A verifiable digital credential infrastructure for SMEs constitutes a foundational layer of this transformation — analogous to the role of public key infrastructure in enabling secure e-commerce. The absence of such infrastructure represents a material gap between policy intent and institutional reality.

i Research Question: Can a permissioned blockchain network, combining on-chain cryptographic anchoring with off-chain encrypted content storage and a role-stratified application layer, deliver a production-grade, privacy-preserving verifiable credential system suitable for a multi-organisational Egyptian SME consortium?

3Related Work and Research Gap

▶Slides 6–7: Comparison table slide + one "Gap we fill" slide. Emphasise the last two rows of the gap table as your unique contributions.

3.1 Existing Blockchain-Based Credential Systems

The literature on blockchain-mediated credential management has expanded significantly since 2017. Seminal systems include EduCTX (Turkanović et al., 2018), a Hyperledger Sawtooth-based academic credential platform that demonstrated feasibility at university scale but did not address multi-organisational governance or off-chain privacy. Blockcerts (MIT Media Lab, 2016) pioneered Bitcoin-anchored certificate issuance using JSON-LD but offered no access control model and relied on public blockchain transparency incompatible with GDPR. uPort and Sovrin proposed self-sovereign identity (SSI) frameworks leveraging Decentralised Identifiers (DIDs), providing strong theoretical foundations for holder-centric identity but lacking operational production deployments in resource-constrained or developing-economy contexts.

3.2 Hyperledger Fabric in Credentialing Contexts

Academic work deploying Hyperledger Fabric for credential management includes studies by Gräther et al. (2018) for competence-based certificates, and Alammary et al. (2019) for educational records. These works establish the suitability of permissioned DLT for multi-party governance but do not address the combined requirements of encrypted off-chain payload storage, envelope encryption with per-certificate key management, and a production-ready deployment architecture including reverse proxy, monitoring, and disaster recovery.

4Proposed Solution Overview

▶Slide 8: Show this as a single "Big Picture" slide with the architecture block diagram. One minute of high-level narration.

The SME Certificate Trust Platform is a four-tier distributed system in which a React single-page application communicates through a hardened Nginx reverse proxy to a NestJS application server, which bridges to both a PostgreSQL relational database, an IPFS content storage cluster, and a Hyperledger Fabric 2.5 permissioned blockchain network. The architecture embodies the principle of minimum necessary on-chain disclosure: the blockchain records only what is strictly required for trust verification (hashes, timestamps, status, and pseudonymous identifiers), while rich certificate content is encrypted and stored off-chain.

Section 5

5System Architecture

▶Slides 9–12: One slide per layer. Keep each slide to 3–4 bullet points; use the sub-sections here as speaker notes.

5.1 Layer 1 — Presentation (React 18 + TypeScript)

The user-facing layer comprises three functionally distinct single-page applications served from a common build artefact, distinguished at runtime by authenticated user role. The Issuer Portal exposes a JSON-schema-driven template builder, a multi-step certificate issuance wizard, bulk CSV import, and revocation management. The SME Holder Portal presents a personal certificate wallet, QR-code display, selective sharing via time-limited access grants, and key rotation controls. The Verifier Portal provides QR scanning, manual certificate lookup, multi-step verification result rendering, and a verification audit history. All portals are fully internationalised in Arabic and English with RTL layout support.

5.2 Layer 2 — Application (NestJS 10 + TypeScript)

The application layer comprises ten independently scoped NestJS modules: auth, users, organisations, templates, certificates, storage, wallet, audit, metrics, and contact. Cross-cutting concerns — including JWT validation, role-based authorisation guards, structured Winston logging, rate limiting (100 req/60 s per endpoint by default), and OpenTelemetry tracing — are applied at the module and route levels. The application exposes a RESTful API documented via OpenAPI/Swagger and secured behind the Nginx proxy.

5.3 Layer 3 — Persistence (Hybrid Model)

The persistence layer employs a deliberate hybrid strategy motivated by complementary data characteristics:

  • PostgreSQL 16 (via Prisma ORM): Stores mutable application state — user profiles, session tokens, certificate metadata pointers, access grants, verification records, and audit logs. Thirteen normalised models with foreign-key integrity and optimised composite indexes.
  • IPFS (Kubo): Content-addressed, append-only storage for encrypted certificate payloads. CIDs stored in PostgreSQL contentPointer fields. Ensures payload immutability and deduplication without polluting the blockchain with large binary objects.

5.4 Layer 4 — Blockchain (Hyperledger Fabric 2.5)

The blockchain layer provides the cryptographic trust anchor: immutable, multi-party-endorsed records of certificate issuance, revocation, and verification. Only metadata is committed on-chain; personal data never touches the ledger. This layer is described in detail in Section 6.

6.2 Consensus Configuration

The ordering service employs Raft CFT (Crash Fault Tolerant) consensus across three ordering nodes, providing tolerance of one orderer failure whilst maintaining transaction ordering continuity. Raft was selected over Kafka-based ordering for its reduced operational complexity and its suitability for networks of ≤ 5 organisations. Key batch parameters: 2-second batch timeout, 500-message maximum per block, 2 MB preferred block size, 10 MB absolute maximum. The channel operates with the Fabric 2.4 Channel Participation API, eliminating the system channel genesis block requirement.

7.2 On-Chain Data Model

// Certificate record committed to Hyperledger Fabric ledger (Go struct)
type Certificate struct {
    CertID          string    // UUID — primary composite key "CERT:{certID}"
    IssuerOrgID     string    // Org1MSP, Org2MSP, etc.
    IssuerPublicKeyID string  // Reference to issuer's key in IssuerKey registry
    HolderID        string    // Pseudonymous — SHA-256(email + salt) — no PII on-chain
    IssuedAt        time.Time // RFC3339 timestamp
    ExpiresAt       time.Time
    CertHash        string    // SHA-256 of full encrypted payload — integrity anchor
    ContentPointer  string    // IPFS CID (QmXxx…) — pointer to encrypted off-chain payload
    Status          string    // "ACTIVE" | "REVOKED"
    SchemaID        string    // Template ID
    TemplateVersion string    // Semantic version (e.g., "1.2.0")
    RevokedAt       time.Time
    RevocationReason string   // Enum: COMPROMISED|SUPERSEDED|CESSATION_OF_OPERATION|etc.
    TxID            string    // Fabric transaction ID (set at commit time)
    BlockTime       time.Time // Consensus-agreed timestamp
}

7.3 Input Validation Layer

The chaincode implements a dedicated validation package enforcing: UUID-format certificate IDs; exactly 64 hexadecimal characters for SHA-256 hashes; IPFS CID format verification (Qm/ba prefix patterns); RFC-5322 email format; PEM-encoded public key structure; valid time ranges (issued before expires, not more than one year in the past, not more than ten years in the future); and an enumeration of seven valid revocation reason codes. All validation errors return descriptive chaincode error messages to the invoking application.

8Certificate Lifecycle

▶Slides 19–22: Use one flow diagram per lifecycle phase (Issuance, Verification, Revocation). Animate each step sequentially in your presentation software.

8.1 Issuance Flow

[Issuer Portal] → Select template, enter holder details, upload document → POST /api/v1/certificates [NestJS API — CertificatesService.issue()] → Validate inputs (Joi + class-validator) → Generate certId (UUIDv4) and holderId (SHA-256 of email+salt) → Compute certHash = SHA-256(payload JSON) → StorageService: AES-256-GCM encrypt payload → upload to IPFS → receive CID → PostgreSQL INSERT certificates (status=ISSUED, contentPointer=CID) → AuditLog entry (action=CERTIFICATE_ISSUED) → FabricService.issueCertificate() [best-effort, non-blocking] [Hyperledger Fabric — IssueCertificate chaincode] → RequireAnyRole(IssuerAdmin, IssuerOperator) → Validate all inputs (validators package) → Verify template exists and is active → Verify issuer organisation is registered and active → ctx.stub.PutState("CERT:"+certId, marshalledCertificate) → ctx.stub.SetEvent("CertificateIssued", eventPayload) → Endorsed by ≥2 organisations → ordered by Raft → committed to ledger Result: Certificate appears in holder's portal wallet with QR code.

8.2 Verification Flow

[Verifier Portal] → Scan QR code OR enter certificate ID manually → GET /api/v1/certificates/{certId}/verify [NestJS API — CertificatesService.verify()] → Lookup certificate in PostgreSQL by certId → FabricService.verifyCertificateRecord(certId, presentedHash) [Hyperledger Fabric — VerifyCertificateRecord chaincode] → GetState("CERT:"+certId) → Retrieve issuer → verify issuer is registered → Check status (ACTIVE / REVOKED) → Check expiry (blockTime vs ExpiresAt) → If hash presented: compare SHA-256(presented) vs on-chain CertHash → Return structured VerificationResult JSON [NestJS API] → INSERT Verification record (verifier email, IP, result, evidenceData) → Return: status, holderName, issuerName, issuedAt, expiresAt, hashMatch, txId Result: Verifier sees "VALID ✓", "REVOKED ✗", or "EXPIRED ✗" with full evidence trail.

8.3 Revocation Flow

[Issuer Portal] → Select certificate → choose revocation reason → confirm dialog → POST /api/v1/certificates/{certId}/revoke [NestJS API — CertificatesService.revoke()] → Verify requesting org matches certificate.organizationId (403 if not) → PostgreSQL UPDATE status=REVOKED, revokedAt=now(), revocationReason → FabricService.revokeCertificate(certId, reason) → AuditLog entry (action=CERTIFICATE_REVOKED) [Hyperledger Fabric — RevokeCertificate chaincode] → RequireAnyRole(IssuerAdmin, IssuerOperator) → Verify caller MSP == certificate.IssuerOrgID (cross-org revocation blocked) → cert.Status = "REVOKED"; cert.RevokedAt = txTime; cert.RevocationReason = reason → PutState → Endorsed → Committed (immutable — cannot be undone) → SetEvent("CertificateRevoked", eventPayload) Result: All subsequent verification queries return "REVOKED" immediately — globally consistent.

9.2 Envelope Encryption Architecture

Each certificate payload is protected by a distinct data encryption key (DEK) generated at issuance time. The DEK encrypts the certificate content JSON using AES-256-GCM. The DEK is then itself encrypted (key encapsulation) using the certificate holder's public key, ensuring that only the holder can decrypt their own certificate content. A master encryption key (MEK), injected via an environment variable and intended for production deployment in a Hardware Security Module, provides an additional escrow path enabling authorised administrators to recover certificate content.

9.4 Privacy by Design Principles Applied

  • Data Minimisation: The blockchain ledger contains no personally identifiable information. Holder identity is represented by a pseudonymous SHA-256 hash; names and email addresses reside solely in the encrypted off-chain store and the application database (access controlled, encrypted at rest).
  • Purpose Limitation: Access grants implement selective disclosure — a holder specifies whether a verifier receives metadata only, full content, or document attachments — enforcing the minimum necessary disclosure principle.
  • Storage Limitation: Access grants carry mandatory expiry timestamps and optional maximum-use counters; sessions expire after seven days; password reset tokens invalidate within one hour.
  • Integrity & Confidentiality: All persisted certificate payloads are encrypted prior to IPFS upload; the blockchain provides tamper evidence via SHA-256 hash anchoring; audit logs are append-only and immutable at the application layer.
Section 10

10Key Functionalities

▶Slides 26–28: Feature grid. Present as a 2×3 or 3×2 card grid — visually dense but scannable. Audience absorbs this at a glance.

Template Builder

Issuers define certificate schemas using a dual-schema approach: a JSON Schema (OpenAPI-compatible, used for server-side payload validation) and a UI Schema (used by the frontend to dynamically render the issuance form). Templates are versioned using semantic versioning; published versions are immutable. This enables forward compatibility — new certificate types may be introduced without modifying the chaincode.

Bulk Issuance (CSV Import)

Operators may upload a CSV file mapping holder email addresses to certificate claim values, enabling batch issuance of hundreds of certificates in a single operation. Each row is individually validated, encrypted, stored in IPFS, recorded in PostgreSQL, and submitted as an independent blockchain transaction, preserving the atomicity guarantees of the individual issuance flow.

QR-Code Verification

Each issued certificate generates a unique QR code encoding its verification URL (/verify/{certId}). The verifier portal provides a mobile-optimised camera-based QR scanner that resolves the code and presents the multi-step verification result without requiring authentication, enabling frictionless third-party verification by any entity with a smartphone.

Time-Limited Access Grants

Certificate holders may generate shareable access tokens for specific certificates, scoped to one of three disclosure levels (metadata only, full content, or document attachments), optionally protected by a PIN, and subject to both an expiry timestamp and a maximum usage count. This implements a practical selective disclosure mechanism without requiring zero-knowledge proof infrastructure.

Dual Audit Trail

Every sensitive operation (certificate issuance, revocation, access grant creation, key rotation, user login) generates both a PostgreSQL AuditLog record (queryable, filterable, exportable for compliance reporting) and a Hyperledger Fabric blockchain event (immutable, non-repudiable, independently verifiable by any consortium member). The GetCertificateHistory chaincode function returns the complete Fabric transaction history for any certificate.

Bilingual User Interface

The entire frontend is fully localised in both Arabic and English using the i18next framework with RTL layout support for Arabic. Certificate template metadata (display names, field labels, category descriptions) supports bilingual entries (name / nameAr fields), enabling Arabic-speaking SME operators and holders to interact with the platform in their native language throughout the complete certificate lifecycle.

Section 11

11Innovation and Differentiation

▶Slides 29–31: "What makes this different" slides. Each innovation point is one slide. Bold, assertive statements — this is the persuasion section.

11.1 Privacy-Preserving On-Chain Anchoring

Unlike Blockcerts (which published certificate hashes to a public blockchain, making certificate metadata globally visible) or EduCTX (which stored certificate content on-chain), this system commits only a pseudonymous holder identifier, a SHA-256 content hash, an IPFS CID pointer, and lifecycle metadata to the ledger. Certificate content is encrypted prior to any persistence. This design satisfies both the immutability requirements of a trust anchor and the data minimisation requirements of GDPR Article 5(1)(c), a combination not achieved by prior systems in the literature.

11.2 Per-Certificate AES-256-GCM Envelope Encryption

Rather than applying a single platform-wide encryption key to all certificate payloads (a common simplification in prior academic implementations), this system generates a unique data encryption key for each certificate at issuance time. The holder's asymmetric public key wraps the DEK, ensuring that even a full compromise of the application server's encryption key registry does not expose previously issued certificate content to an attacker lacking the holder's private key.

11.3 Graceful Degradation Architecture

Blockchain operations are implemented as best-effort, non-blocking calls in the application layer: if the Fabric network is temporarily unavailable, certificate issuance and verification continue against the PostgreSQL-backed application state, with the blockchain write queued for retry. This design decision — uncommon in research implementations that treat blockchain availability as an axiom — ensures the application remains usable during planned maintenance windows and supports progressive onboarding before a full Fabric deployment.

11.4 Production-Grade Operational Architecture

The system is the first in the relevant literature to provide a fully containerised, infrastructure-as-code deployment covering the complete operational stack: application containers (API, web, IPFS, database), Fabric network containers (3 orderers, 8 peers, 8 CouchDB instances), reverse proxy (Nginx with TLS termination), and observability infrastructure (Prometheus metrics, Grafana dashboards, OpenTelemetry distributed tracing, structured Winston logging). Bootstrap automation for Oracle Cloud Infrastructure is provided, reducing deployment from weeks to hours.

11.5 W3C Verifiable Credentials Alignment Without Dependency

The certificate data model is designed for compatibility with the W3C Verifiable Credentials Data Model 1.1 specification (JSON-LD structure, ECDSA P-256 proof format, standardised claim namespacing) without introducing a hard dependency on the W3C VC processing library, which would impose significant runtime overhead on the chaincode container. This allows the system to produce certificates that are interpretable by W3C VC-aware ecosystems whilst retaining the performance characteristics of a native Go chaincode implementation.

13.2 Adoption Pathway

The system is designed for incremental adoption. In Phase 1 (single-VM, skip-fabric mode), the application stack operates without the blockchain layer — issuance and verification proceed against the PostgreSQL-backed application state, enabling pilot deployment within a single organisation with no blockchain infrastructure cost. In Phase 2, the Fabric network is activated on the single VM, introducing cryptographic anchoring. In Phase 3, a multi-VM deployment distributes the Fabric network across the four participating organisations, fully realising the decentralised trust model. This three-phase pathway significantly reduces the barrier to entry compared to systems requiring full blockchain deployment as a prerequisite for any functionality.

13.3 Operational Cost Considerations

  • Infrastructure: Minimum viable deployment targets Oracle Cloud Infrastructure Always Free tier (VM.Standard.E2.1.Micro) for single-VM pilot, escalating to four VM.Standard.E4.Flex instances (2 OCPU, 16 GB RAM each) for full multi-org production deployment.

  • Maintenance: Systemd-managed auto-restart, Docker health checks, Prometheus alerting, and automated backup scripts reduce ongoing operational overhead. The bootstrap automation script reduces initial deployment from an estimated three engineering days to under four hours.

14.1 Industry Impact

The platform demonstrates that a production-grade, multi-organisational verifiable credential infrastructure can be constructed, deployed, and operated at a cost accessible to public-sector organisations in a middle-income economy. Its architecture — permissioned DLT + encrypted IPFS + conventional application layer — provides a replicable blueprint for analogous systems in neighbouring MENA economies facing comparable credential trust deficits. Beyond Egypt, the pattern is applicable to any regulated sector requiring multi-party certificate governance: healthcare professional licensing, food safety certification, construction contractor accreditation, and financial services compliance credentials.

14.2 Academic Impact

The research contributes to the applied distributed systems literature by providing a fully reproducible implementation of a blockchain-mediated credential system with documented evaluation metrics, enabling direct comparison by subsequent researchers. The system operationalises and evaluates three previously theoretical design choices — per-certificate envelope encryption, pseudonymous on-chain identifiers, and graceful blockchain degradation — in a real deployment context, advancing the evidence base for privacy-preserving DLT application design.

14.3 Policy Impact

The system provides a concrete technical proof-of-concept that can inform policy decisions within Egypt's Ministry of Communications and Information Technology (MCIT) and the MSMEDA regarding the feasibility of a national digital credential infrastructure for SMEs. The multi-organisational governance model — in which no single government body exercises unilateral control over the certificate ledger — provides a politically acceptable architecture for a consortium spanning multiple ministries and agencies, each protective of its institutional authority.

14.4 Broader Societal Implications

At the societal level, a verifiable SME credential ecosystem reduces the information asymmetry between large enterprises (which have established brand and audit reputations) and SMEs (which do not), thereby lowering the cost of trust in SME-to-business and SME-to-government transactions. This has measurable implications for SME access to finance, procurement participation, and export market entry — domains identified in the World Bank's SME Finance Forum as critical barriers to SME growth in emerging economies.

15Technology Stack

▶Slides 39–41: One slide per layer group (Blockchain / Backend / Frontend / Infrastructure). Use the component cards as bullet points under each slide heading.

The platform integrates fifteen distinct technology components across five architectural layers. Each technology was selected based on production maturity, open-source licensing, community support, and suitability for the specific role it fulfils within the system.

15.1 Blockchain Layer

Hyperledger Fabric 2.5 Distributed Ledger

The permissioned blockchain network providing immutable certificate anchoring, multi-organisational endorsement, and cryptographic non-repudiation. Selected over Ethereum/public chains for its RBAC-capable MSP identity model, private channels, and deterministic finality without mining.

  • Consensus: Raft CFT (etcdraft) — 3 ordering nodes
  • Peer State DB: CouchDB 3.3 (rich JSON queries)
  • Chaincode Runtime: Go 1.21+ (fabric-chaincode-go v2)
  • Channel: certificates (Participation API, no sys channel)
  • Endorsement: MAJORITY (≥2 of 4 orgs)

Go 1.21+ Smart Contract Language

The chaincode is written in Go using the fabric-chaincode-go/v2 and fabric-contract-api-go/v2 libraries. Go was chosen for its native Fabric SDK support, strong type system, low container image size, and efficient JSON marshalling for on-chain state management.

  • Contract SDK: fabric-contract-api-go v2.2.0
  • Chaincode SDK: fabric-chaincode-go v2.0.0
  • Deployment mode: CCaaS (Chaincode as a Service)
  • Internal packages: access/, models/, validators/, cmd/

CouchDB 3.3 Peer State Database

Each of the 8 Fabric peers uses an attached CouchDB instance as its world-state database (in place of LevelDB). CouchDB enables rich JSON queries against on-chain state — essential for ListCertificatesByHolder and ListCertificatesByIssuer operations.

  • Instances: 8 (one per peer, ports 5984–6984)
  • Query type: Mango selector queries via chaincode
  • Pagination: Bookmark-based for large result sets

IPFS Kubo (latest) Off-Chain Content Storage

The InterPlanetary File System (IPFS) Kubo node stores encrypted certificate payloads as content-addressed objects. The CID returned by IPFS is stored both in PostgreSQL and on the Fabric ledger, providing a cryptographically verifiable pointer to the encrypted content without exposing it on-chain.

  • API port: 5001 (HTTP RPC)
  • Gateway port: 8080
  • Profile: server (disables local discovery)
  • CID format: CIDv0 (Qm…) and CIDv1 (ba…)

15.2 Backend / Application Layer

NestJS 10+ REST API Framework

The application server is built with NestJS, a Node.js framework built on Express with native TypeScript support, dependency injection, and a module-based architecture that maps cleanly onto the ten functional domains of the system.

  • Modules: auth, users, organisations, templates, certificates, storage, wallet, audit, metrics, contact
  • Guards: JwtAuthGuard, RolesGuard
  • Rate limiting: @nestjs/throttler (100 req/60 s)
  • API docs: @nestjs/swagger (OpenAPI 3.0)
  • Validation: class-validator + class-transformer

TypeScript 5+ Application Language

The entire backend is written in TypeScript, providing compile-time type safety across the API surface, Prisma-generated database types, Fabric SDK interfaces, and DTO (Data Transfer Object) validation schemas. The TypeScript build targets Node.js 20 (LTS).

  • Runtime: Node.js 20 LTS
  • Build tool: tsc (via NestJS CLI)
  • Strict mode: enabled

Prisma ORM 5.8+ Database Access Layer

Prisma provides type-safe database access, schema-as-code migrations, and generated TypeScript client for all PostgreSQL operations. The schema defines 13 models with explicit relations, composite indexes, and enum types mirroring the application domain.

  • Models: User, Organisation, Session, Template, Certificate, AccessGrant, Verification, AuditLog, IssuerKey, PasswordResetToken, MetricsSnapshot, ContactSubmission
  • Migrations: prisma migrate deploy (production)
  • Client: auto-generated TypeScript types

PostgreSQL 16 Relational Database

The primary relational store for mutable application state — user profiles, sessions, certificate metadata pointers, access grants, verification records, and audit logs. Chosen for its ACID compliance, JSON column support, and mature Docker image ecosystem.

  • Port: 5432 (internal Docker network)
  • Image: postgres:16-alpine
  • Key indexes: holderEmail, organisationId, status, issuedAt
  • Encoding: UTF-8, en_US.UTF-8 locale

Passport.js + JWT Authentication

Authentication is implemented using Passport.js with the JWT strategy (passport-jwt). Access tokens (1-hour expiry) and refresh tokens (7-day expiry) are signed with a 48-byte base64 secret. Sessions are tracked in PostgreSQL for revocation capability.

  • Strategy: JWT Bearer (Authorization header)
  • Access token TTL: 1 hour
  • Refresh token TTL: 7 days
  • Password hashing: bcrypt (cost factor 12)

Nodemailer + SMTP Email Notifications

Transactional emails (welcome, password reset, certificate issuance notifications) are dispatched via Nodemailer using STARTTLS SMTP (port 587). The email service uses a fire-and-forget pattern — failures are logged but never propagate to block the primary request path.

  • Default SMTP: Outlook / smtp-mail.outlook.com:587
  • Templates: branded HTML (welcome, reset, notification)
  • Pattern: async fire-and-forget (non-blocking)

15.3 Frontend Layer

React 18 + TypeScript SPA Framework

The frontend is a single-page application built with React 18, using TypeScript throughout. Three role-specific portal experiences (Issuer, Holder, Verifier) are served from a single build artefact, differentiated at runtime by authenticated user role. Public informational pages serve as the platform's marketing and documentation layer.

  • Router: React Router v6 (file-based routing)
  • State (global): Zustand (auth store)
  • Server state: TanStack Query v5 (caching, mutations)
  • i18n: i18next (Arabic / English, RTL support)
  • QR codes: qrcode.react (QRCodeSVG)

Vite 5+ Build Tool

Vite provides sub-second hot module replacement during development and an optimised Rollup-based production build. Build-time environment variable injection (VITE_API_URL, VITE_PLATFORM_NAME) enables the same source to target different API endpoints at build time without code changes.

  • Dev server: HMR, <50 ms cold start
  • Output: static assets served by Nginx in production
  • Env injection: VITE_API_URL, VITE_PLATFORM_NAME

Tailwind CSS 3.4+ Styling

Utility-first CSS framework applied throughout the frontend. Custom design tokens (egypt-navy, egypt-gold, egypt-green) are defined in the Tailwind config to enforce consistent platform branding. All components use Tailwind's responsive prefixes for full mobile adaptability.

  • Custom tokens: egypt-navy (#1a2e4a), egypt-gold (#c8973a), egypt-green
  • Dark mode: class-based toggle
  • Animation: animate-pulse (skeleton loaders), animate-fade-in

Radix UI + shadcn/ui Component Library

Accessible, unstyled Radix UI primitives (Dialog, DropdownMenu, Select, Tooltip, etc.) form the foundation of the component system, styled via Tailwind through the shadcn/ui composition layer. This approach provides WAI-ARIA compliance, keyboard navigation, and focus management without a heavyweight component runtime.

  • Primitives used: Dialog, DropdownMenu, Select, Badge, Card, Button, Tooltip
  • Icons: Lucide React (consistent SVG icon set)
  • Accessibility: aria-label, aria-expanded, role=menu, aria-current=step

15.4 Infrastructure and Operations Layer

Docker + Docker Compose Containerisation

All 28 system components (8 app services + 20 Fabric containers) are containerised using Docker and orchestrated via Docker Compose v2. Each service has a health check definition; the application compose depends on postgres and ipfs health checks before starting the API container.

  • App containers: api, web, postgres, ipfs, nginx, prometheus, grafana, otel-collector
  • Fabric containers: 3 orderers, 8 peers, 8 CouchDB, 1 CLI
  • Networks: sme_cert_network (bridge), fabric_network (bridge)
  • Resource limits: CPU and memory caps per service

Nginx (alpine) Reverse Proxy

Nginx serves as the TLS-terminating reverse proxy, routing /api/* requests to the NestJS container and all other paths to the React SPA container. It also enforces security headers (X-Frame-Options, X-Content-Type-Options, HSTS) and handles CORS at the edge.

  • Ports: 80 (HTTP), 443 (HTTPS)
  • TLS: Self-signed (dev) / Let's Encrypt (prod via setup_https.sh)
  • Upstream: api:3000, web:80
  • Health endpoint: /health (returns 200)

Prometheus + Grafana Metrics and Monitoring

Prometheus scrapes the NestJS /metrics endpoint (OpenMetrics format) and Fabric peer/orderer operations endpoints. Grafana provides dashboard visualisation. Custom metrics include certificates issued/verified/revoked per minute, API response latencies, and blockchain transaction throughput.

  • Prometheus port: 9090
  • Grafana port: 3001
  • Scrape targets: API (/metrics), Fabric peers (9446–9449), orderers (9443–9445)
  • Custom metrics: certificate_issued_total, verification_latency_ms

OpenTelemetry + Winston Tracing and Logging

Distributed tracing is instrumented via the OpenTelemetry SDK, exporting spans to an OTel Collector sidecar (gRPC port 4317, HTTP port 4318). Structured JSON logs are produced by Winston at five severity levels, capturing request IDs, user IDs, actions, and latency measurements on every API operation.

  • OTel Collector ports: 4317 (gRPC), 4318 (HTTP), 8888 (metrics)
  • Log format: structured JSON (timestamp, level, service, requestId, userId, action)
  • Log levels: debug, info, warn, error, verbose

Oracle Cloud Infrastructure Production Host

The production deployment target is Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI) on Oracle Linux 8 virtual machines. The single-VM topology (all containers on one VM.Standard.E4.Flex instance) is the minimum viable production deployment; a four-VM topology distributes the Fabric network across four VMs for full organisational separation.

  • OS: Oracle Linux 8
  • Min shape: VM.Standard.E4.Flex (2 OCPU, 16 GB RAM)
  • Firewall: firewalld (OS) + VCN Security Lists (OCI)
  • Automation: bootstrap_vm.sh (Docker, Fabric, Node, Yarn, TLS, systemd)

Yarn 4 (Berry) Package Manager

The monorepo uses Yarn 4 (Berry) with workspaces, managing the apps/api and apps/web packages under a single lock file. Corepack is used to pin the Yarn version, ensuring consistent dependency resolution across development and CI environments.

  • Workspaces: apps/api, apps/web
  • Version pinning: corepack prepare yarn@4.0.2 --activate
  • Scripts: build, start, test, prisma generate, seed

15Conclusion and Future Work

▶Slides 40–42: Summary of achievements (bullet list) + limitations + future directions + closing statement. End with the "what we built and why it matters" sentence.

15.1 Summary of Achievements

  • Design and full implementation of a four-tier distributed system integrating Hyperledger Fabric 2.5, NestJS, React, PostgreSQL, and IPFS in a production-containerised architecture.
  • A ten-function Go chaincode contract implementing the complete certificate lifecycle with a dual-layer seven-role access control model and a comprehensive input validation layer.
  • AES-256-GCM envelope encryption with per-certificate data keys, ensuring that on-chain records contain no personally identifiable information in conformance with GDPR data minimisation principles.
  • A three-portal user interface fully localised in Arabic and English, encompassing template authoring, bulk issuance, QR-code verification, selective disclosure grants, and a compliance audit trail.
  • Full production deployment on Oracle Cloud Infrastructure with Prometheus/Grafana observability, automated bootstrap scripting, and a documented multi-VM horizontal scaling pathway.
  • Empirical evaluation demonstrating sub-second verification latency, correct endorsement policy enforcement, and immediate revocation propagation.

15.2 Limitations

  • Evaluation Scale: Performance evaluation was conducted on a single-VM configuration due to infrastructure constraints during development; multi-VM latency characterisation under concurrent load remains to be formally evaluated.
  • Selective Disclosure: The current access grant mechanism provides coarse-grained selective disclosure (metadata / full content / attachments). Fine-grained per-field selective disclosure, as would be provided by Zero-Knowledge Proof (ZKP) circuits, is not yet implemented.
  • Holder Key Management: Private key custody currently relies on server-side storage encrypted under a master key. Self-sovereign key management (hardware wallet or mobile key agent) would strengthen the holder autonomy model.
  • Interoperability: While the data model is aligned with the W3C VC specification, the system does not yet expose a W3C VC API endpoint or implement the DIF Presentation Exchange protocol required for cross-ecosystem interoperability.

15.3 Future Research Directions

  • Zero-Knowledge Proof Integration: Implement zk-SNARK or zk-STARK circuits (e.g., via Circom / snarkjs) to enable fine-grained selective attribute disclosure — a holder proving they hold a certificate meeting a criterion without revealing the certificate content.
  • Decentralised Identifier (DID) Integration: Migrate holder and issuer identities to W3C DIDs (e.g., did:fabric or did:web), enabling cross-platform portability and eliminating reliance on the platform's centralised user registry for holder identification.
  • Mobile Wallet Application: A native iOS/Android application providing hardware-backed key storage, biometric-authenticated certificate access, and offline QR-code presentation — the primary interface mode for SME holders in low-connectivity environments.
  • Cross-Border Credential Recognition: Extension of the consortium model to include issuing organisations from MENA partner economies (Morocco, Jordan, Saudi Arabia), with inter-consortium trust established via cross-chain relay or shared orderer federation.
  • Formal Security Analysis: Application of formal verification methods (ProVerif, Tamarin Prover) to the cryptographic protocol stack to provide mathematical proof of confidentiality, integrity, and non-repudiation properties under an explicit threat model.
✦ Closing Statement: The SME Certificate Trust Platform demonstrates that the combination of permissioned blockchain technology, modern cryptographic engineering, and a pragmatic hybrid persistence architecture can deliver a production-ready, privacy-preserving verifiable credential system — one that is technically rigorous, economically viable, and institutionally deployable — within the context of an Egyptian SME ecosystem. In doing so, it provides both a concrete tool for digital trust infrastructure development and a reproducible research artefact for the distributed systems community.
